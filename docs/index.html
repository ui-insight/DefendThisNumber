ENVS 5010 guest lecture, third in a series

Can You Defend This Number?

Truth, verification, and trust in AI-assisted research. AI accelerates the pipeline, but it does not change who is accountable for what comes out the other end. If you cannot trace a claim back to its source, it is not ready to share.

Demo work draws on AI4RA, supported by NSF grant #2427549

A tour of three demos

If you cannot trace it, do not share it.

Demo 1

Measuring extraction accuracy

Push a real NSF solicitation through Vandalizer, the AI4RA team's document intelligence engine. Compare the extracted requirements against ground truth, side by side. See exactly where AI is right, where it goes sideways, and how much you would have to verify by hand.

Method: ground-truth comparison. Lesson: trust the eval, not the output.

Open Demo 1
Demo 2

Reproducibility and non-determinism

The same prompt, run three times, returns three different answers. Show what temperature means in practice and why this breaks the citation contract.

Point: "the AI said so" is not a defensible source.

Open Demo 2
Demo 3

End-to-end provenance trace

Take a single claim and trace it from raw data, through AI processing, to a published assertion. Show where each step is defensible and where it is not.

Point: provenance is a chain, and AI inserts links you have to vouch for.

Vandalizer

Document intelligence engine

The AI4RA team's document intelligence engine. Open a PDF, write a prompt, get structured output back. Compare the output against ground truth to measure how trustworthy it is. Featured in Demos 1 and 2.

Mindrouter

Prompt execution and provenance logging

Every AI run, every output, every evaluation, traceable end to end. Where the receipts live, and how we answer "where did this number come from?" Featured in Demo 3.
